The PKI Trust Paradox

May 10, 2025 2 min read Pepijn van der Stap PKI, RFC, Security

We've built the internet's security on a foundation of trust that we can't verify. The Certificate Authority system-defined in RFC 5280 and extended by RFC 6962-is probably flawed by design.

How many CAs does your browser trust? More importantly, how many people have the power to mint certificates for any domain? The answer: we don't even know. New CAs are added silently. Sub-CAs spawn more sub-CAs. Cross-signing creates webs of trust that no one fully maps.

Consider this: A single compromised CA-anywhere in the world, under any jurisdiction-can issue a valid certificate for your bank. For your government. For your infrastructure. The RFC 5280 path validation doesn't care about geography, politics, or intent. It only cares about signatures.

Certificate Transparency (RFC 6962) was supposed to fix this. Make all certificates public. Catch misissuance. But it's a band-aid on a broken system. We're logging the symptoms, not curing the disease.

The real issue? Trust doesn't scale. Every CA added to the root store is another point of failure. Every intermediate certificate is another key that could be stolen. Every OCSP responder is another service that could lie.

We are the only folks positioned to change this. Not by adding more complexity, but by recognizing that the PKI model assumes a world that no longer exists-one where trust was scarce, certificates were expensive, and CAs were few.

The future isn't more CAs. It's systems that assume compromise, verify constantly, and trust nothing. We need to stop patching RFC 5280 and start building what comes next.

The Reality of Critical Infrastructure

May 2, 2025 3 min read Pepijn van der Stap ICS, SCADA, Protocols

Every week introduces a new botnet, yet another CVE, and rumors of more zero-days. But beneath the headlines, critical infrastructure often runs on systems that lack the protection they deserve.

SCADA edge nodes parsing raw UDP with unchecked pointers. ICS ports opened during backbone transit, no authentication. Full trust. Fragmented protocols, dialects that aren't standardized. Even Wireshark maintainers feel the strain-it's becoming nearly impossible to maintain parsers with evolving protocols and growing complexity. And let's face it, proprietary, old protocols aren't exactly debugged often.

This is the reality behind the systems that keep our most critical infrastructure running. We need protocol engineers who can identify vulnerabilities others miss. Individuals who can read net RFCs like bytecode; who write parsers that are reliable under load; who challenge assumptions and move past simple wrappers around problems.

Attackers don't seek undefined behavior in system protocols; they exploit the blind spots created by design, standardization, and deliberate neglect.

Metrics? CVEs. Reality: Protocols.

If your job involves reverse-engineering firmware, fuzzing undocumented stacks, or still wrestling with legacy encodings, you are not behind. You are early, and you are tackling the work that truly moves the needle.
